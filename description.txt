We will create an annotated dataset of open-access clinical case report abstracts labeled for perceived diagnostic clarity from a lay reader’s perspective. 
The goal is to study how clearly the diagnostic process is communicated in medical narratives, without requiring medical expertise from annotators.

Each abstract will receive two categorical labels.

To answer staff comments on AP1, we discuss labeling scheme in depth here. We added more details for the labels and lessened the scope by having annotators focus
only on case description and conclusion.



Label 1: Perceived Diagnostic Clarity

Clear – The diagnosis is presented confidently and directly, with little or eveb no discussion of diagnostic difficulty or alternative explanations.

Example: “A diagnosis of appendicitis was made based on imaging findings and the patient underwent surgery.” (There's no ambiguity at all)

Somewhat uncertain – The abstract includes some ambiguity or competing possibilities, but the diagnostic story is still mostly resolved.

Example: “The patient initially presented with symptoms suggestive of viral meningitis, but further testing supported a diagnosis of bacterial meningitis.”

Highly uncertain – The abstract emphasizes diagnostic difficulty, such as misdiagnosis, prolonged diagnostic workup, or conflicting evidence.

Example: “After several years of misdiagnosis and multiple inconclusive tests, the patient was ultimately diagnosed with primary angiitis of the central nervous system.”



Label 2: Primary Source of Diagnostic Uncertainty

Annotators will identify the main factor contributing to uncertainty in the narrative:

1. Diagnostic revision or misdiagnosis

2. Multiple competing diagnoses

3. Conflicting or inconclusive tests

4. Delayed recognition or prolonged diagnostic workup

5. Unusual symptom presentation

6. No clear source of uncertainty

7. Other

To reduce noise from background medical information, annotators will focus on the case description and conclusion portions of the abstract, 
where the diagnostic narrative is typically described.

Although certain phrases (e.g., “initially suspected,” “misdiagnosed,” “inconclusive”) may correlate with uncertainty, labels cannot be assigned using simple keyword rules. 
Annotators must evaluate the overall tone and narrative context to determine how clearly the diagnostic process is communicated.

Data will be sourced from the PubMed Central Open Access Subset, which contains scientific articles released under licenses that permit reuse and redistribution. 
Only abstracts from articles with compatible licenses will be included, and the dataset will track PMCID identifiers and license information to ensure compliance.
